    Question 1

    (1) Researchers created a list of facts that about 50% of people knew. Subjects in this experiment read the list of facts and had to say which ones they knew. They then had to judge what percentage of other people would know those facts. Researchers found that the subjects responded differently about other people’s knowledge of a fact when the subjects themselves knew that fact. If the subjects did know a fact, they said that an inaccurately large percentage of others would know it too. For example, if a subject already knew that Hartford was the capital of Connecticut, that subject might say that 80% of people would know this, even though the correct answer is 50%. The researchers call this finding “the curse of knowledge.”

    • The researchers claim that this “curse” happens because subjects have trouble switching their point of view to consider what someone else might know, mistakenly projecting their own knowledge onto others.

    • 2. The researchers claim that this “curse” happens because subjects make more mistakes when they have to judge the knowledge of others. People are much better at judging what they themselves know.

    • 3. Brain scans indicate that “curse” happens because of the frontal lobe brain circuitry known to be involved in self-knowledge. Subjects have trouble switching their point of view to consider what someone else might know, mistakenly projecting their own knowledge onto others.

    • 4. Brain scans indicate that this “curse” happens because of the frontal lobe brain circuitry known to be involved in self-knowledge. Subjects make more mistakes when they have to judge the knowledge of others. People are much better at judging what they themselves know.

    Question 2

    (2) Subjects read a fact about a type of bird (for example, “All sparrows have livers”) and had to decide whether this fact was true of all birds. For half of the facts, the birds used were stereotypical members of the category (sparrows, robins), and the birds were more rare for the other half of the facts (peacocks, penguins). The researchers found that subjects were more likely to answer correctly when the facts used stereotypical birds than when the facts used rare birds, even though logically there should be no difference. They replicated this effect with other kinds of animals, such as mammals and reptiles. They conclude that people are better at reasoning about stereotypical animals than rare animals.

      1. Researchers claim that people perform this way because information about stereotypical animals is more readily accessed and manipulated than information about rare animals.

    • 2. Researchers claim that people perform this way because the facts that used rare animals were harder for people to answer correctly, leading to more incorrect answers on these questions.

    • 3. Researchers determined that people perform this way by examining the patterns of activation in subjects’ brains as they performed this task. Information about stereotypical animals is stored in a certain way by CA3 brain cells, which have been shown to mediate memory. This makes the information more readily accessed and manipulated than information about rare animals.

    • 4. Researchers determined that people perform this way by examining the patterns of activation in subjects’ brains as they performed this task. The facts that used rare animals were stored in a certain way by CA3 brain cells, which have been shown to mediate memory. This makes it harder for people to answer correctly, leading to more incorrect answers on these questions.

    Question 3

    (3) Babies were seated on their mothers’ laps in front of a stage. Researchers used a camera to track where the babies were looking. The babies saw a hand reach out and place one doll on the stage. Then a screen was raised, hiding the doll. A hand reached out again and placed a second doll on the stage, out of sight behind the screen. Then the screen dropped. In some cases, there were two dolls on the stage, as there should be, and in some cases there was only one doll. The researchers found that the babies looked much longer at the stage when there was only one doll than when there were two dolls. This looking-time difference between one doll and two dolls lead the researchers to conclude that babies can calculate 1 + 1 = 2.

    • 1. The researchers claim this happens because the babies had formed an expectation about how many dolls there should be on the stage. The babies knew there should be two dolls, and their surprise at seeing only one led to their looking longer.

    • 2. The researchers claim this happens because the amount of time the babies spent looking at the stage is directly proportional to how much they liked the display. The researchers used this timing data to calculate babies’ preference for the single doll.

    • 3. The researchers claim that scans of the babies’ brains show that this happens because the part of babies’ brains known to be involved in math, the parietal lobe, governed the babies’ expectations about how many dolls there should be on the stage. The babies knew there should be two dolls, and their surprise at seeing only one led to their looking longer.

    • 4. The researchers claim scans of the babies’ brains show that this happens because the part of babies’ brains known to be involved in math, the parietal lobe, governed the amount of time the babies spent looking at the stage. This time is directly proportional to how much they liked the display, and the researchers used this timing data to calculate babies’ preference for the single doll.

    Question 4

    (4) Subjects watched two short videos involving a pair of animated balls. In one video, the balls moved randomly around the screen. In the other video, one ball appeared to chase the other around the screen. The researchers analyzed subjects’ descriptions of the two videos. They found that the subjects couldn’t stop referring to intentions, desires, and goals in their descriptions of the “chasing” video. The subjects used different types of words, usually related to motion, when describing the other video. The researchers conclude that people think about goal-directed action differently than random action.

    • 1. Researchers claim that this difference occurs because, when people see motion that appears to be goal-directed, they automatically attribute beliefs and desires to the object performing that motion, even if they know that it’s been programmed to move that way.

    • 2. Researchers claim that this difference occurs because one video was explicitly programmed to look like the balls were chasing each other, and this encouraged the subjects to describe these balls as behaving differently from the balls in the random- motion video.

    • 3. Researchers claim that this difference occurs because subjects’ brains showed different patterns of activation in the two conditions. Area MT of the visual system is able to interpret different kinds of motion, so when people see motion that appears to be goal-directed, they automatically attribute beliefs and desires to the object performing that motion, even if they know that it’s been programmed to move that way.

    • 4. Researchers claim that this difference occurs because subjects’ brains showed different patterns of activation in the two conditions. Area MT of the visual system is able to interpret different kinds of motion, so when one video was explicitly programmed to look like the balls were chasing each other, this encouraged the subjects to describe these balls as behaving differently from the balls in the random-motion video.

    Question 5

    (5) Subjects sat in front of a computer screen and watched a rapidly presented series of pictures, half of which were faces and half of which were places. Half of the subjects had to press a button each time they saw a face, and the other half of the subjects pressed a button for places. Researchers analyzed the response times and discovered that the patterns of response times were different for faces than for places.

    • 1. The researchers claim this happens because people use different processes to recognize faces than they use to recognize places.

    • 2. The researchers claim this happens because the subjects’ responses were contingent on whether they saw a face or a place on the screen.

    • 3. An examination of the activation patterns in subjects’ brains shows that the extrastriate cortex (known to be involved in processing complex visual stimuli) uses different processes to recognize faces than they use to recognize places.

    • 4. An examination of the activation patterns in subjects’ brains shows that the extrastriate cortex (known to be involved in processing complex visual stimuli) responded contingently on whether they were responding to a face or a place on the screen.

    Question 6

    (6) A group of three-year-old children participated in this task. They were shown a set of five objects, four of which were familiar to them and one of which was new. An experimenter asked them to point to each of the familiar objects, which they did correctly. Then the experimenter asked them to point to the “blicket,” a name that the children had not heard before. The children reliably pointed to the novel object and not to any of the familiar objects.

    • 1. The researchers claim that children applied the new name to the unfamiliar object because children assume that a single object can have only one name. All of the other objects already had names, so they applied the word “blicket” to the new object.

    • 2. The researchers claim that children applied the new name to the unfamiliar object because the word “blicket” was new to the children. They needed to pick one of the objects to which to apply this name, and therefore chose the new object.

    • 3. Researchers examined children’s brain activation as they performed this task, and concluded that children applied the new name to the unfamiliar object because of the way brain’s language area in the left temporal lobe assigns labels. Children assume that a single object can have only one name. All of the other objects already had names, so they applied the word “blicket” to the new object. 

    • 4. Researchers examined children’s brain activation as they performed this task, and concluded that children applied the new name to the unfamiliar object because of the way brain’s language area in the left temporal lobe assigns labels. The word “blicket” was new to the children. They needed to pick one of the objects to which to apply this name, and therefore chose the new object.

    Question 7

    (7) Subjects saw a picture of a complex object made out of wooden cubes glued together. After looking at this object, they saw a new picture, which was either a different object entirely or the first object rotated so that it was seen from a different angle. Subjects had to judge whether this second object was the same one as the first. The researchers discovered that subjects responded faster to objects that have been rotated only a few degrees, and subjects take longer to respond when the object has been rotated more. The researchers discovered that the time it takes subjects to respond to the test question is proportional to how much the object has been rotated.

    • 1. The researchers claim that this result shows that subjects are rotating the object in their imagination before answering the question.

    • 2. The researchers claim that this result shows that objects rotated only a few degrees elicited quicker responses from the subjects.

    • 3. The researchers claim that brain scans of these subjects show that subjects are rotating the object in their imagination before answering the question. This may be the result of a specific group of brain processes in the occipital lobe, an area known to be involved in mental imagery.

    • 4. The researchers claim that brain scans of these subjects show that objects rotated only a few degrees elicited quicker responses from the subjects. This may be the result of a specific group of brain processes in the occipital lobe, an area known to be involved in mental imagery.

    Question 8

    (8) A group of Democrats and a group of Republicans viewed campaign ads for both Democratic and Republican candidates. The subjects then answered a series of questions about the content of the ads. Researchers found that all subjects showed highly negative response patterns to the ads for the opposite party, but highly positive response patterns to their own party’s ads.

    • 1. The researchers claim that this happens because political party affiliation affects how people think about candidates.

    • 2. The researchers claim that this happens because people tend to avoid responding with praise to opposite-party ads.

    • 3. By examining subjects’ brain activation as they performed this task, researchers concluded that this happens because of how a brain structure called the amygdala, known to be involved in emotional processing, responds in this task: political party affiliation affects how people think about candidates.

    • 4. By examining subjects’ brain activation as they performed this task, researchers concluded that this happens because of how a brain structure called the amygdala, known to be involved in emotional processing, responds in this task: people to tend to avoid responding with praise to opposite-party ads.

    Question 9

    (9) A group of subjects over the age of 60 saw a 4 x 4 grid on a computer screen. Some of the squares in the grid contained pictures. After two minutes, the grid disappeared. Subjects then engaged in three tests. First, they were shown a set of pictures and were asked if any of the pictures had been in the original grid (object test). Second, they were shown a grid with dots in some of the squares and asked if any of these squares had contained pictures in the original grid (location test). Third, they saw a grid with pictures in some of the squares and were asked whether any of those pictures had appeared in those locations in the original grid (object + location test). Researchers found that the subjects were good at the first two tests but performed poorly on the third test.

    • 1. The researchers claim that this happens because, as we get older, we have trouble remembering multiple pieces of linked information at once.

    • 2. The researchers claim that this happens because, as we get older, we retain the ability to perform well on some specific types of tasks but not others.

    • 3. Brain scans indicate that this happens because, as we get older, the memory centers in the medial temporal lobe change in specific ways. We therefore have trouble remembering multiple pieces of linked information at once.

    • 4. Brain scans indicate that this happens because, as we get older, the memory centers in the medial temporal lobe change in specific ways. We therefore retain the ability to perform well on some specific types of tasks but not others.

    Question 10

    (10) A group of white subjects saw pictures of white faces and black faces on a computer screen in a random order. For each face, subjects had to judge whether it was the same as or different from the face that came before it. Subjects also filled out a questionnaire about their contact with people of other races. Researchers found that the subjects were better at recognizing the white faces than the black faces in general. They also found that subjects with less other-race contact were worse at recognizing the black faces. They conclude that it is more difficult in general to recognize faces of races different from one’s own, which they call “the other-race effect.”

    • 1. The researchers claim that the other-race effect is caused by a lack of experience with other races.

    • 2. The researchers claim that the other-race effect is caused by an inability to recognize faces of other races.

    • 3. Scans of the subjects’ brains show that the other-race effect is caused by the way the fusiform face area in the brain responds to faces when people lack experience with other races.

    • 4. Scans of the subjects’ brains show that the other-race effect is caused by the way the fusiform face area in the brain responds to faces when people are unable to recognize faces of other races.

    Question 11

    (11) Subjects sat at a computer screen. They saw a rapidly flashing series of pictures of faces. Somewhere in this series of faces there were two pictures of houses. Subjects had to press a button each time they saw a house. When the two houses were far apart in the sequence, the subjects were very good at this task. But when the houses were presented close together in the sequence, subjects failed to press the button for the second house. The researchers call this phenomenon “attentional blink.”

    • 1. The researchers claim that this phenomenon occurs because the subjects were still processing the first house and missed seeing the second house because they did not have enough attentional resources left.

    • 2. The researchers claim that this phenomenon occurs because the second house appeared later in the sequence than the first house, and this temporal relationship between the two houses caused the attentional blink.

    • 3. Researchers examined subjects’ brain activation as they performed this task. They concluded that this phenomenon occurs because of how areas in the frontal lobe, previously shown to mediate attention, functioned in response to the stimuli. The subjects were still processing the first house and missed seeing the second house because they did not have enough attentional resources left.

    • 4. Researchers examined subjects’ brain activation as they performed this task. They concluded that this phenomenon occurs because of how areas in the frontal lobe, previously shown to mediate attention, functioned in response to the stimuli. The second house appeared later in the sequence than the first house, and this temporal relationship between the two houses caused the attentional blink.

    Question 12

    (12) A group of subjects performed the same set of cognitive tests twice, once while thinking about God and once while thinking about a famous person. The researchers found that, when the subjects were thinking about God, their test results showed the same response pattern typical of religious tendencies regardless of their personal beliefs. The results showed a different cognitive pattern when they weren’t thinking about God.

    • 1. The researchers conclude that this happens because the tendency to believe in God is universal and innate in all people, and will therefore manifest itself in the same way in all people.

    • 2. The researchers conclude that this happens because thinking about God causes a certain pattern of responses that is distinct from the pattern caused by thoughts about other people.

    • 3. Researchers conclude that this happens by examining the activation in subjects’ brains as they performed this task. The tendency to believe in God is universal and innate in the right prefrontal cortex, an area devoted to forming beliefs. It will therefore manifest itself in the same way in all people.

    • 4. Researchers conclude that this happens by examining the activation in subjects’ brains as they performed this task. Thinking about God causes a certain pattern of responses in the right prefrontal cortex, an area devoted to forming beliefs. These are distinct from the patterns caused by thoughts about other people.

    Question 13

    (13) Researchers tested subjects using a linguistic association task. Subjects heard a target word and had to say the first word that came to mind. The researchers discovered that words spoken within the first 500 ms after hearing the target were words that sounded like the target word. Words spoken after the first 500 ms were words that had a similar meaning to the target word.

    • 1. The researchers conclude that this happens because people categorize words first according to their sounds and later according to their meanings.

    • 2. The researchers conclude that this happens because two different types of words were associated with the target word at two different times.

    • 3. Patterns of brain activation in these subjects lead researchers to conclude that this happens because Broca’s area, a part of the brain’s language system, categorizes words first according to their sounds and later according to their meanings.

    • 4. Patterns of brain activation in these subjects lead researchers to conclude that this happens because Broca’s area, a part of the brain’s language system, associates two different types of words with the target word at two different times.

The researchers determined that men are better at spatial reasoning in general. From the interviews, they discovered that the men had played more sports in their childhood on average than the women.

    • 1. The researchers conclude that the difference in involvement in sports explains the gender difference in spatial reasoning abilities.

    Question 16

    (16) Subjects were asked to imagine a series of objects that were make-believe (for example, a unicorn) or that were real but not present in the room (for example, a mountain). As the subjects created mental images of the various objects, they were asked questions about their images and told to respond as quickly as possible, without reflecting on their answers. They were also asked the same questions about objects they could actually see in the room (for example, a pen). From an analysis of the responses to these questions and of the times it took subjects to respond, the researchers found a similar pattern of responses and response times for all three types of objects.

    • 1. The researchers claim that this happens because imagining an object, whether real or make-believe, uses the same process as seeing a real object.

    Question 17

    (17) A group of subjects were given a pair of 3D glasses which had one red lens and one green lens. As they looked at a special picture through the red lens only, they saw a house. Through the green lens, they saw a face. During the study, the subjects looked at the picture with both eyes, one looking through the red lens and one through the green lens. Scientists asked them to press a button to report what they were currently seeing over a period of several minutes. The scientists discovered that the two images switched off — sometimes people saw a face, sometimes a house, but never both at once. This phenomenon is called “binocular rivalry.”

    • 1. The researchers claim that binocular rivalry happens because we can only process one complete image at a time. 

    • 2. The researchers claim that binocular rivalry happens because the two images alternate with each other over time.

    • 3. Patterns of activation in subjects’ brains indicate that binocular rivalry happens because we can only process one complete image at a time in V1, the visual area of the brain. 

    • 4. Patterns of activation in subjects’ brains indicate that binocular rivalry happens because the two images alternate with each other over time in V1, the visual area of the brain.

    Question 18

    (18) Subjects were asked to examine a list of facts and study them for a later test (the studying phase). During this studying phase, they also had to say all their thoughts aloud as scientists recorded their speech with sophisticated voice-analysis software. Subjects were later given a test on the facts that they studied. When the scientists examined the subjects’ voices from the studying phase, they found that there was a different pattern of vocal activity when a subject later remembered the fact than when the subject did not.

    • 1. The researchers claim that this happens because the process we go through while learning information is the primary factor that affects our memory of that information. 

    • 2. The researchers claim that this happens because some types of vocal activity during the studying phase are correlated with memory for the information being studied. 

    • 3. The researchers claim that brain scans from these subjects show that the process we go through while learning information is important. This process is the primary factor that affects the way the information is stored in and retrieved from the hippocampus, the primary brain area involved in memory.

    • 4. The researchers claim that brain scans from these subjects show that some types of vocal activity during the studying phase are important. This activity is correlated with the way the information is stored in and retrieved from the hippocampus, the primary brain area involved in memory.
